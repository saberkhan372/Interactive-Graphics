From Apple II to WebGL

Interactive Graphics was a two-year Dalton elective, taught across roughly 19 weeks and 38 to 51 class meetings per year, that traced computer graphics from the Apple II to modern real-time 3D. The course was built around a simple claim: the limits of a machine shape the art, code, and culture made with it.

This site reconstructs that course as a public archive. It keeps the personal origin of the class, the historical sequence, and the hands-on tools together because the intellectual work is the same in each place: make the hidden system visible, name how it works, and then build something more intentional with that knowledge.

Where this started

"My first memories of computers are also about computer graphics and the impact it has on humans... I spent the first 8 years of my life in Bangladesh where I did not see a computer. I remember my father, an architect, rendering designs on AutoCAD on his Intel i386 PC... Around that time my aunt in the United States sent us a Nintendo Entertainment System... the 8-bit graphics and innovative game design got me hooked."

— from the course welcome letter, September 2022

Apple II, NES, Bryce3D, and what each era's hardware would and wouldn't let you make.
Unit 1

BASIC and Fractals

Applesoft BASIC on an emulator, bouncing balls, and a recursive fractal tree.
Unit 2

2D to 3D

The bridge from flat shapes to WebGL, adding a third dimension on purpose.
Unit 3

Pipeline and Wireframe

The math under WebGL: building a 3D renderer without the library doing it for you.
Unit 4

3D Scenes and WebGL

Lighting, materials, camera, and custom shapes, built into a full scene piece by piece.
Unit 5

Game Dev Bridge

Pixel art skills meet GB Studio through sprites, palettes, and a first playable game.
