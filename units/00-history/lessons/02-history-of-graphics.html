Unit 0 · Lesson 0.2

History of Computers to Pixels

The lesson gives pixels an intellectual history. Boolean logic, the bit, early programmable computers, raster scanning, vector drawing, Sketchpad, Spacewar, and wireframes all become part of the same story: graphics depends on how a machine stores, addresses, and changes information.

Source deck: 0.2 - History of Graphics.pptx · 26 slides

What the class did

Students began by naming inventions and concepts that predate computer graphics, then followed a timeline from George Boole and Claude Shannon through Alan Turing, John Tukey's bit, the SEAC, Sensorama, the first digital scanner image, Bézier curves, Sketchpad, Spacewar, early 3D simulation, Beflix, the mouse, Boeing Man, hyperlinks, touchscreens, and the term pixel.

The class returned to the graph-paper pixel activity at the end. The historical timeline was not background information; it prepared students to ask what a pixel is, how a system identifies it, and why a picture can be treated as data.

Key idea

A pixel is not just a tiny square. It is a decision about sampling, storage, position, and display. Once an image can be broken into addressable elements, it can be copied, transmitted, transformed, and programmed.
