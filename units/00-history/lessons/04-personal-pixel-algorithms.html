Unit 0 · Lesson 0.4

Personal Pixel Algorithms

This lesson keeps technical precision and personal meaning together. Students write instructions for recreating their own image, then use research to ask what kinds of people, communities, and institutions shape creative coding.

Source deck: 0.4 - Personal Pixel Algorithms.pptx · 7 slides

What the class did

The warm-up asked how graphics performance is measured, what affects performance, and why people care about it. Students then prepared a typed algorithm for someone else to recreate their graph-paper design, with numbered steps and attention to pixel identity, location, color, and order.

The second activity moved into research. Students looked at books, journals, and web resources connected to Dorothy Santos, Xiaowei Wang, the Processing Foundation, p5.js, and Processing Foundation writing. The structure asked them to mark what felt compelling, intriguing, or intimidating, then turn that response into questions.

Key idea

A graphics course can ask two questions at once: how does the image work, and who is the work for? The same lesson that makes students specify pixels also asks them to notice the social and artistic context around the tools.

Try it

Write one rule in the Pixel Algorithm Playground that feels connected to your original graph-paper design. Keep the rule short enough that another person could explain it back to you.
